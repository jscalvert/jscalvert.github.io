The first disproof of a prediction about DLA

This post highlights recent work which, for the first time, rigorously rules-out a prediction made about diffusion-limited aggregation (DLA), a paradigmatic model of random, dendritic growth.

The model üîó

DLA is a random process which grows a connected subset, or cluster, of the two-dimensional Euclidean lattice $\mathbb{Z}^2$, one element at a time.1 It was introduced by Witten and Sander in 1981 and serves to model a variety of physical phenomena, such as electrodeposition and Hele-Shaw flow.2 Start with a set $A_0 = \{o\}$ consisting of the origin $o$. One step of the dynamics is given by $$ A_{t+1} = A_t \cup \{X\},$$ where $X$ is the first site in the boundary of $A_t$ visited by a simple random walk “from infinity.” In other words, DLA is a Markov chain with transition probabilities (known in this context as growth probabilities) given by $$ P (A_{t+1} = A_t \cup \{x\} \bigm\vert A_t) = H_{\partial A_t} (x), $$

where $H_{\partial A_t}$ is the harmonic measure of $\partial A_t$,3 and where $\partial A_t$ denotes the boundary of $A_t$, i.e., elements $y$ which do not belong to $A_t$, but are at distance $| y - x| = 1$ from some element $x$ which does.

State-of-the-art simulations show that large DLA clusters have a cross-shaped backbone with many smaller scales of branching, the confluence of which forms narrow channels, called “fjords” (Figure 1).4 Since the “tips” of the cluster are exposed, their boundaries have greater growth probabilities than those at the bottom of the fjords. The multifractal conjecture concerns the way these growth probabilities scale with the linear size (i.e., the diameter) of the cluster.

DLA is difficult to analyze üîó

Before discussing the multifractal conjecture in greater detail, it is worth highlighting the extent to which DLA has evaded rigorous study. Despite being introduced more than 40 years ago, there are only two non-trivial, rigorous results about DLA on $\mathbb{Z}^2$. The first, due to Kesten,5 states that there is a positive number $C < \infty$ such that, almost surely, for all sufficiently large $t$, the linear size $L(t)$ of the cluster at time $t$ satisfies $$ L(t) \leq C t^{\frac{2}{3}}. $$ The second, due to Eberz-Wagner,6 states that, almost surely, the DLA cluster will have infinitely-many holes.

The multifractal conjecture üîó

What it means for the smallest growth probability üîó

Informally, the assertion that DLA is multifractal means that the growth probabilities on the boundary of a typical cluster exhibit power-law scaling with the linear size of such a cluster.7 In particular, concerning the least positive growth probability, $p_{min}$, we can make “typical” precise via expectation, by defining $$ \log p_{min} (t) = E \big[ \log \min \{ H_{\partial A_t} (x): H_{\partial A_t} (x) > 0 \} \big]. $$ Note that, in the absence of the expectation, $p_{min} (t)$ would simply be the random quantity $$ \min \{ H_{\partial A_t} (x): H_{\partial A_t} (x) > 0 \}. $$

In terms of the expected linear size $L(t)$ of a DLA cluster at time $t$, multifractality asserts that there is a positive number $\alpha < \infty$ such that $$ p_{min} (t) \approx L(t)^{-\alpha}, $$ where we write $f(t) \approx g(t)$ to mean that there are positive real numbers $c$ and $C$ such that $$ c g(t) \leq f(t) \leq C g(t) $$ for all sufficiently large $t$. For example, suppose there is a $\beta \in [\frac12,1]$ such that $L (t)$ almost surely scales as $L (t) \approx t^{\beta}$.8 In this case, $p_{min} (t)$ must satisfy $$ p_{min} (t) \approx t^{- \beta \alpha}. $$

What is predicted about $\alpha$ and $\beta$? üîó

Concerning $\beta$, nearly every (non-rigorous) analytical study has predicted $\beta = \frac35$,9 while nearly every simulation study has predicted a slightly smaller value of approximately $\beta = \frac{1}{1.71}$.10 The fact that these separate consensuses appear to be at odds is interesting but, for the purposes of our discussion, it suffices to note that it is widely believed that $\beta > \frac12$. In other words, it is believed that the DLA cluster forms branches like those in Figure 1 and, in particular, the cluster is not a disk.

Concerning $\alpha$, the controversy seems to be far greater—it is unclear if $\alpha$ exists at all! Indeed, while a pair of studies11 claimed to have definitively established a finite value of $\alpha$, they were based on simulations of DLA clusters over only tens of thousands of steps, whereas the later simulations of Grebenkov and Beliaev suggest that the cluster’s shape changes dramatically over 100 times as many steps. Additionally, many (at least nine, by my count) earlier studies predicted that $p_{min} (t)$ decays faster than any inverse power of $L(t)$, and it is unclear how the concerns raised by these analytical studies are addressed by the simulations which claim a finite $\alpha$.12

What if multifractality fails? üîó

If multifractality fails for $p_{min} (t)$, meaning that no finite $\alpha$ satisfies $$ p_{min} (t) \approx L(t)^{-\alpha}, $$ then we can ask: How rapidly does $p_{min} (t)$ decrease, as a function of $t$? My recent work with Shirshendu Ganguly and Alan Hammond partly addresses this question.13 Translated into this context, our result states that there is a constant $C$ such that, for all sufficiently large $t$, $$ \log p_{min} (t) \geq -C L(t). $$ Hence, the least positive growth probability can be no smaller than exponential in the linear size of the cluster. For example, this rules-out the prediction made by Lee and Stanley14 that $$ \log p_{min} (t) \approx -L(t)^2, $$ as well as the form predicted by Blumenfeld and Aharony,15 $$ \log p_{min} (t) \approx - \frac{L(t)^c}{\log L(t)}, $$ when $c > 1$.

To my knowledge, this is the first instance of a rigorous result ruling-out predictions made about DLA in the physics literature.
