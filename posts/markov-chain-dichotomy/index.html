Markov chain dichotomy and collective motion

Jul 13, 2022 · 536 words · 3 minute read

This post explains how the Markov chain dichotomy of transience and recurrence implies dichotomous behavior of diameter for certain models of collective motion.

One way to model the motion of a collective with $n$ individuals is as a Markov chain on $n$-element subsets of the vertex set of a graph.1 For example, consider the state space $$ \Omega := \{ A \subset \mathbb{Z}^d: |A| = n\} $$ for positive integers $d$ and $n$.

If only the relative positions of the individuals matter, then it is natural to view the elements of $\Omega$ up to translation. Specifically, define the equivalence relation $\sim$ on $\Omega$ according to $A \sim B$ if $A$ is a translate of $B$, i.e., $$ A \sim B \quad \text{if} \quad \exists \, z \in \mathbb{Z}^d: B = \{a+z: a \in A\}. $$ Denote by $\widehat{A}$ the class of elements of $\Omega$ which are translates of $A$ and define $\mathrm{diam} (\widehat{A})$ to be the diameter of any representative of the class $\widehat{A}$.

A standard result in Markov chain theory states that an irreducible Markov chain $$ (X(0), X(1), \dots) $$ on a countable state space $\Sigma$ is either transient or recurrent. Define the number of visits it makes to $\sigma \in \Sigma$ by $$ V (\sigma) := \sum_{t=0}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{\{ X(t) = \sigma\}}. $$ Transience means that $$ \forall \, \sigma \in \Sigma, \quad \mathbb{P} \big( V(\sigma) = \infty \bigm\vert X(0) = \sigma \big) = 0, $$ while recurrence means $$ \forall \, \sigma \in \Sigma, \quad \mathbb{P} \big( V(\sigma) = \infty \bigm\vert Y(0) = \sigma \big) = 1. $$

Assume that $$ (U(0), U(1), \dots) $$ is an irreducible Markov chain on $\Omega$, in which case $$ (\widehat{U}(0), \widehat{U}(1), \dots) $$ is an irreducible Markov chain on $\widehat{\Omega}$.

Suppose that $(\widehat{U}(t))_{t\geq 0}$ is transient. For any real number $r$, there are only finitely many classes in $\widehat{\Omega}$ with diameter of $r$ or less, i.e., in $$ \{ \widehat{\omega} \in \widehat{\Omega}: \mathrm{diam} (\widehat{\omega}) \leq r \}. $$ By the transience condition, these finitely many states are each visited only finitely many times almost surely, hence $$ \forall \, \widehat{\omega} \in \widehat{\Omega}, \quad \mathbb{P} \big( \liminf_t \mathrm{diam} (\widehat{U} (t)) > r \bigm\vert \widehat{U} (0) = \widehat{\omega} \big) = 1. $$ Since the event in question is decreasing in $r$, this implies $${\color{blue} \forall \, \widehat{\omega} \in \widehat{\Omega}, \quad \mathbb{P} \big( \liminf_t \mathrm{diam} (\widehat{U} (t)) = \infty \bigm\vert \widehat{U} (0) = \widehat{\omega} \big) = 1.} $$

Suppose instead that $(\widehat{U}(t))_{t\geq 0}$ is recurrent. While the diameter of $\widehat{U} (t)$ may become arbitrarily large, it nevertheless visits states with finite diameter infinitely often. Hence $${\color{blue} \forall \, \widehat{\omega} \in \widehat{\Omega}, \quad \mathbb{P} \big( \liminf_t \mathrm{diam} ( \widehat{U} (t) ) < \infty \bigm\vert \widehat{U} (0) = \widehat{\omega} \big) = 1.} $$

The two blue displays show how the dichotomy of transience and recurrence implies a dichotomy in the behavior of diameter for Markov chains which naturally arise in the study of collective motion.
